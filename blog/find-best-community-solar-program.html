How to Find the Best Community Solar Program in Your State

March 2025  ·  10 min read

Community solar sounds simple — subscribe to a solar farm, get credits on your bill. But when you start researching, you quickly discover there are dozens of programs competing for your attention, each claiming to be the best option. Discount rates vary. Contract terms vary. Some programs are genuinely great. Some aren't. This guide gives you the framework to tell the difference.

What Actually Makes a Good Community Solar Program?

Strip away the marketing language and a good community solar program comes down to four things:

  • Guaranteed discount rate — not a range, not a projection. A guaranteed minimum percentage off your electricity supply rate.
  • Clear, fair contract terms — you should understand what you're committing to, including length and exit options.
  • Simple cancellation — life changes. You should be able to leave with reasonable notice and no penalty.
  • Utility compatibility — the farm must be in your utility territory. A great program in the wrong territory is useless to you.

The Discount Rate: What to Look For

The discount rate is the percentage below the utility's retail rate that you'll pay for the solar energy credited to your account. When comparing programs, always look for:

  • Guaranteed vs. estimated — some programs advertise "up to 25%" but guarantee only 5%. Look for the guaranteed floor.
  • Fixed vs. floating — a fixed discount rate gives you predictable savings. A floating rate tied to utility rate changes can mean more savings if rates rise, but also uncertainty.
  • What the rate applies to — most discounts apply only to the electricity supply portion of your bill, not delivery charges or taxes. That's normal, but important to understand.

📊 A 10% guaranteed discount is better than a "15–25% estimated" discount with no floor. Always ask for the contract minimum.

Contract Terms: Questions to Ask Before You Sign

  • What is the contract length? (1 year vs. 5+ years makes a big difference)
  • What is the cancellation policy and notice period?
  • Are there any early termination fees?
  • What happens if I move out of the utility territory?
  • Is my subscription size fixed or does it auto-adjust to my usage?
  • Who do I contact if my credits are missing or incorrect?
  • Is there a waitlist, and if so, how long?

Red Flags to Avoid

🚩 "Up to X% savings" with no guaranteed minimum — a common bait-and-switch tactic.

🚩 Multi-year lock-in contracts (5+ years) with steep early termination fees.

🚩 Programs that require you to switch your utility account entirely.

🚩 No clear contact information for billing disputes.

🚩 Pressure to enroll quickly due to "limited availability" — a common sales tactic. Good programs have waitlists, not countdowns.

Why Shopping Alone Is Harder Than It Looks

The challenge with shopping for community solar yourself is that most programs aren't marketed directly to consumers. They're marketed through aggregators, door-to-door sales, and comparison sites — many of which earn commissions from specific providers regardless of which program is actually best for you.

Additionally, availability changes constantly. A farm might show up on a comparison website but already be fully subscribed by the time you call. Program terms are often buried in PDFs and vary significantly from what's advertised.

State-Specific Considerations

Program quality and availability vary significantly by state. States with strong regulatory oversight (Maryland, New York, Massachusetts, Minnesota) tend to have more consumer protections built into program requirements. States where community solar is newer may have fewer guardrails.

In states with mandatory minimum discount rates, you're protected by law. In others, the terms are entirely determined by the developer — making vetting even more important.
